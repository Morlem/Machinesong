BACKGROUND

This was the seven month capstone course for the Human-Comuter Interaction Masters program at Carnegie Mellon. I was the project coordinator for a team of four working on a website redesign for the American Institutes of Research (AIR), one of the largest behavioral and social science research organizations in the world.

Feel free to contact me for more information about my role in this project or our process. We produced two very substantial reports: one for the research phase and one for the final design.

EXPLORE

Following the contextual design process, we started this project by conducting contextual inquiries and less formal interviews. In total we spoke to 21 individuals through on-site and remote interviews. This data, along with our findings from a competitive analysis and other research, informed the redesign of AIR’s site.

After the research phase, my team and I used that information to fuel an initial ideation phase where we generated ideas to best address our stakeholders’ needs. Our vision was to build a new web presence for AIR that accomplished three primary goals:

  1. showcase the expertise and capabilities of AIR and its employees
  2. radiate the humanity of the individuals that make up the organization
  3. function as a valuable information resource for policymakers, media, other researchers, and practitioners in the fields where AIR works


Our final solution accomplished the goals above with a few key features. One was adding employee profiles to the site that, in addition to showing professional accomplishments, gave the people of AIR a public face and voice. We also strongly interconnected the existing content to facilitate an opportunistic browsing experience where visitors could easily jump to related news, research, researchers, and more.

BUILD

Wireframes

Leading a workshop

I sketched designs on paper to get initial feedback and then started creating low-fidelity paper mock-ups in Balsamiq. After the team and I generated a number of alternatives, we picked the best ideas and refined our designs by getting feedback from other students in the Human-Computer Interaction Institute.

For the next iteration of our design we created more detailed wireframes in Adobe Fireworks. This allowed us to add more detail and easily create interactivity while still iterating quickly by reusing elements in multiple designs.

Finally, we created a low-fidelity and high-fidelity working prototypes of the new site using HTML, CSS, Javascript, and Python. We refined the prototype after a final round of think-aloud usability tests.

We traveled to D.C. to deliver a final presentation to the communications team and the president of AIR. We delivered a high-fidelity functional prototype, the demo video below, and a detailed report of our process and findings.

LESSONS LEARNED

Know your team. Every team is different and will face unique challenges. Every member of our team was a hard worker, but occasionally someone would put in a lot of effort without keeping the rest of the team informed. This sometimes created situations where someone wanted to share input and feedback and never had the opportunity. We dealt with this by working in the same space as often as possible and by sharing frequent updates.

Know your client. Never having been responsible for a project like this before, this was a huge lesson. It was our job to communicate with our client and understand their needs, sometimes despite what they thought those needs were. It was challenging at first, but ultimately we were able to deliver something that we were proud of and in a way that was satisfying to our client and gave them a clear path forward.
